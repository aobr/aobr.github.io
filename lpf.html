Stellar and Bremstrahlung radiation effects in simulated galaxies on-the-fly

To a large extent, numerical studies of galaxy formation assume a uniform, isotropic and time-dependent radiation field, known as the ultraviolet background (UVB). This is because coupling the hydrodynamic equations with the radiation transfer equation slows down simulations by orders of magnitude and also requires excessive memory allocation. While the UVB approximation is reasonable on large scales, it is definitely not appropriate inside galaxies, where the radiation field varies wildly depending on factors such as the spatial distribution of the most massive young stars, which produce a large proportion of the UV photons. Another source of UV photons is hot plasma that emits free-free radiation.

I have implemented an inverse ray tracing scheme in the N-body smoothed particle hydrodynamics code Gasoline2, counting stellar and gas UV photon sources, and taking the local radiation field (LRF) into account in the energy equation through the heating and cooling terms. In the optically thin approximation, where absorption effects only occur in the vicinity of the source and/or sink, the radiation field can be efficiently computed using the same oct-tree structure primarily used to compute gravitational forces at each particle position. The figure below shows how the radial profile of the HI photoionisation rate changes over time/redshift in a simulated galaxy which has the mass of the Milky Way at the current epoch. The saturated values at large radii correspond to the UVB rates at the times shown in the figure. It is clear from this figure that the HI photoionisation rate inside the galaxy is significantly higher than the UVB level throughout most of this galaxy's history.


HI photoionization rates of a Milky Way-like simulated galaxy. Credit: Obreja et al. (2019)


Taking these radiation effects into account on-the-fly results in measurable differences in the galaxies and their environments. The figure below shows how the column density of three different ions (HI, OVI and OVII with ionizing energies of 13.6eV, 138.1eV and 739.3eV) are affected by the inclusion of the local radiation field (bottom vs top panels).


HI, OVI and OVII column densities around a simulated Milky Way-like galaxy with (bottom) and without (top) local radiation field effects included.


To get similar precision for the radiation field as for the gravitional one with an inverse ray tracing scheme on a Barnes-Hut tree code we have two options. The first one is to construct a different tree for the radiation sources with a smaller threshold θ. The second option, and our choice, is to use efficient higher order multipoles and stick to a one-tree for both gravity and radiation.
