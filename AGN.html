AGN radiation effects in simulated galaxies

Accreting supermassive black holes, also known as active galactic nuclei (AGN), emit copious amounts of ultraviolet (UV) and X-ray photons that can significantly impact the surrounding gas, from the small interstellar medium (ISM) to the circumgalactic medium (CGM) and beyond. In simulations, this largely unknown physical phenomenon is implemented in two ways: as a release of thermal energy, or as a momentum boost to nearby gas particles/cells. The direct effect of AGN radiation on the heating and cooling terms in the energy equation is usually ignored. In a recent paper, we demonstrated that this radiation effect alone can significantly alter the balance between heating and cooling in the CGM of a massive simulated galaxy at redshift z = 3. Overcooling of the CGM is suppressed by a factor depending on the solid angle through which this radiation can propagate (corresponding to the vertex angle α of a bi-cone).


The mass budget of a simulated galaxy's CGM as a function of the vertex angle where AGN radiation can propagate. As α increases (α=0o corresponds to no AGN radiation, while α=180o to isotropic AGN radiation) less/more of the CGM is cooling/heating. These effects depend also on how massive is the black hole and how strong is the accretion (λ=Lbol/LEdd). Figure adapted from 2024MNRAS.527.8078O

AGN radiation also affects CGM observables. By combining observations of various line emissions with absorption studies, we demonstrated that it may be possible to constrain the intrinsic properties of the CGM (density, temperature, composition, and ionisation state) and the properties of the AGN radiation source (mass, accretion rate, and vertex angle of the ionisation cone). To realise the full potential of this idea, I have created a large database of AGN photoionisation models for use in simulations and in an inference pipeline that constrains the CGM and AGN properties of real galaxies in tandem.
